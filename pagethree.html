Roman Siege Engines

The romans did not employ Siege engines until late in the game. The romans first use siege towers in 204 BCE. After that they made changes to them. Such as they made them smaller so they could move them. The romans also added battering rams and boarding bridges. These boarding bridges also came with platforms to hold people and artillery. Thanks to this they could have hand to hand combat even if they were not inside the walls. Siege towers had wheels that allowed them to roll so Rome could build them at a safe distance and then push them closer to attack. In the siege of Gaul Julius Caesar used a 10 story siege tower with platforms that held artillery on them. Caesar rolled the tower up an embankment to cut the enemy off from there fresh water source. These towers looked so fears that in some instances the enemy would rather surrender then fight them. One problem with these towers is that they could be set on fire. So to prevent this they would soak hides in vinegar and cover the wood, or they would use steel parts. The only problem with that is they would then be harder to roll because they were heavier. The siege engines are like modern day bridge tanks. They are tanks with a foldable bridge that extends across a cavern or anything that needs to be crossed. The bridge tanks are built in the home country and brought to the battlefield. Without these machines the armies job would be much harder. It makes the probability for defeating the enemy without taking innocent lives, the army can now get heavy artillery to any play they need, witch gives them precision while taking out the enemy. The romans made this invention to aid them in the battlefield and today it does the same for the American Military.
